Know Your Prospect

The Corporate Champion
Who They Are & What They Need

Before you send a single email, understand the person on the other end. They're not cynical — they're accountable. They want to give. They need a reason to justify it, a story to tell their team, and a partner who looks credible enough to stand behind.

Who They Are
Director of Community Affairs, VP of Marketing, HR Director, or Business Owner
Company Size
Mid-to-large DFW businesses — law firms, financial institutions, healthcare groups
Decision Authority
Controls or heavily influences the charitable giving / CSR budget
Best Time to Reach
ESG reporting cycles, employee giving season, Q4 budget flush, community visibility goals
What They Fear
  • Looking disorganized or financially mismanaged
  • Spending CSR budget on something employees won't care about
  • Getting lost in the crowd — logo on a banner, nothing more
  • Being asked to commit before they understand what they're buying
  • Not being able to measure or explain the ROI internally
What They Need to Hear
  • "Your team will be in the room. Your logo will be seen."
  • "55 years. 100% volunteer-run. Every dollar goes to the work."
  • "Last year's event raised $51,500. Here's exactly what it funded."
  • "Here's what your tier looks like — in print, online, and on-site."
  • "Your team can participate directly — not just write a check."

The Cadence

Three Stages.
One Curated Journey.

This is not a blast campaign. Each email reveals a little more of the story, builds a little more context, and earns a little more trust. By the time you make the ask, they already know who The Arc is — and why it matters to their community.

If they reply at any point — stop the sequence and have a real conversation. The stages below are only for contacts who haven't responded yet.

1
Day 1 · First Contact
The Warm Story
Introduce the mission. Make them feel something. Ask for nothing.
Goal: Open the door. This email is not about sponsorship — it's about the people The Arc serves. You are not selling a package. You are inviting them into a story.
To: [First Name Last Name] — named stakeholder, not a shared inbox
CC: [Internal Champion] if found on LinkedIn
Subject: Something is happening in North Texas this September
Dear [First Name], Somewhere in North Texas right now, there is a young man learning to speak up for himself for the first time. A woman navigating a system that has never been designed with her in mind. A family wondering if the life they want for their child is actually possible. The Arc of DFW Area exists for them. For over 55 years — entirely volunteer-run — The Arc has been the organization that shows up when the system doesn't. Fighting for policy change, connecting families to resources they didn't know existed, and building a community where individuals with intellectual and developmental disabilities can live life as they define it. On September 27th, we're bringing together community leaders, advocates, and businesses at Puttshack in Addison for Putts for Progress — our third annual fundraiser. Every dollar raised goes directly to expanding education, self-advocacy programs, and access to resources for individuals with IDD across 9 DFW counties. "Putts for Progress isn't just a fundraiser. It's a signal — that businesses in this community believe every person deserves a voice in their own life." I'd love to share more about what this event means to the families we serve — and what it could mean for [Company Name]. James Lunday President · The Arc of DFW Area 817.372.3914 · thearcofdfw.org
Guidance Notes
  • Personalize with the contact's first name — never "To Whom It May Concern"
  • If you found an internal champion on LinkedIn, CC them — it signals social proof before the first reply
  • Do not attach the sponsorship deck in Stage 1 — that comes in Stage 3
  • Do not include pricing or tier information — this is a story, not a proposal
  • Send Tuesday through Thursday, 8–10am or 1–3pm — avoid Monday mornings and Friday afternoons
⏳
Wait 5–7 business days. If they reply — move the conversation forward naturally and skip the remaining stages. If no reply — send Stage 2.
↓
2
Day 8–10 · Follow-Up
The Relationship Ask
Low pressure. Give them more context. Invite a conversation.
Goal: Surface the opportunity without pressure. You're not asking for a commitment — you're asking for 20 minutes. Make it easy to say yes to a conversation.
To: [First Name] — same thread or new email
Subject: Following up — a partnership worth 20 minutes
[First Name], I wanted to follow up on my note from last week about Putts for Progress and The Arc of DFW Area. I know your inbox is full — so I'll be brief. We have a small number of sponsorship opportunities available for our September 27th event at Puttshack in Addison. Based on what [Company Name] stands for in this community, I think there's a meaningful fit here. Our sponsors don't just write a check. They show up. Their logo is in the room, their name is in every communication we send, and their team gets to be part of an afternoon that matters to hundreds of families across North Texas. Our event has raised $51,500 over its first two years. This year, we're aiming higher — and we want the right partners in the room. Would any time this week or next work for a 20-minute call? I'd love to walk you through what a partnership could look like for [Company Name] — no pressure, just a conversation. James Lunday President · The Arc of DFW Area 817.372.3914 · thearcofdfw.org
Guidance Notes
  • Keep it short — they didn't reply to Stage 1, so respect their time
  • Reference [Company Name] specifically — this is not a template blast
  • The ask is a conversation, not a commitment — "20 minutes" is the lowest-friction yes possible
  • Still no pricing or deck — that comes only after they express interest
  • If you have a mutual connection, mention it: "I believe you know [Name] — they're a longtime supporter of The Arc."
⏳
Wait 5–7 business days. If they reply with interest — schedule the call and send the deck in advance. If no reply — send Stage 3.
↓
3
Day 15–18 · Final Touch
The Personalized Close
Make the package feel like it was built for them. One clear next step.
Goal: Close the conversation or get a clear no. Name a specific sponsorship tier. Make it easy to say yes — and graceful to say not yet. This is the last email.
To: [First Name]
Subject: One last note — and a specific idea for [Company Name]
[First Name], I don't want to crowd your inbox — so this will be my last note before the event. I've been thinking about what a partnership between [Company Name] and The Arc of DFW Area could actually look like. Based on [what I know about your company / your focus on community / your team's values], I think the [Recommended Tier] sponsorship is the right fit. Here's what that means for [Company Name]: [Insert 3–4 bullet points from the Tier Close Angles section below — written in your own words, not copy-pasted from the deck] The full details and commitment form are at putts4progress.com/sponsorships — or I'm happy to walk you through it directly. If the timing isn't right this year, I completely understand. I'd still love to stay in touch — the work The Arc does is ongoing, and there will be other opportunities to get involved. Either way, thank you for your time. The families we serve don't take it for granted — and neither do I. James Lunday President · The Arc of DFW Area 817.372.3914 · thearcofdfw.org
Guidance Notes
  • Recommend one specific tier — don't present all three; decisiveness signals confidence
  • Write the benefits conversationally — pull from the Tier Close Angles below, not the deck verbatim
  • The "not right this year" paragraph is intentional — it keeps the door open for 2027
  • Include the commitment form link: putts4progress.com/sponsorships — make it one click to yes
  • After this email, no more follow-up this cycle — mark the contact for re-engagement in January 2027

When They Push Back

Objection Handling
What to Say When They Say No

A "no" is rarely final. Most objections are requests for more information, more time, or a different frame. Use these to keep the conversation alive without being pushy.

"Our charitable giving budget is already committed for this year."
Your Response
Completely understood — and I appreciate you being straightforward. If it helps, our commitment form allows for check payments, which some partners find easier to route through end-of-year or Q1 budget cycles. Either way, I'd love to keep The Arc on your radar for 2027 — our event grows every year, and early partnerships get the best visibility. Would it be okay if I reached back out in January?
"We get a lot of sponsorship requests — how is this different?"
Your Response
That's fair — and I'll give you a direct answer. Most sponsorships put your logo somewhere and call it done. This one puts your team in a room with families who are navigating one of the hardest things a family can face. The Arc has been doing this for 55 years, entirely volunteer-run. We don't have overhead eating your donation. And at Putts for Progress, your company isn't a footnote — you're a named partner in a community event people actually talk about.
"I'm not sure our team has a personal connection to this cause."
Your Response
That's more common than you'd think — and it changes the moment someone meets the community we serve. One in six people in the U.S. has an intellectual or developmental disability. Statistically, someone on your team, or someone they love, is navigating this. What we've found is that when a company shows up for this cause, employees who never mentioned a personal connection come forward. The event tends to mean more than the company expected — and that's usually the start of a longer relationship.
"Can you send us more information first?"
Your Response
Absolutely — I'll send the sponsorship details now. The full breakdown is also at putts4progress.com/sponsorships. Take a look and let me know if a quick call would help — most partners find that 20 minutes answers everything they need to make a decision. Happy to work around your schedule.
"What's the ROI on something like this?"
Your Response
Honest answer: the measurable ROI is brand visibility, community association, and employee engagement — harder to put a number on than an ad buy, but more durable. Your logo will be seen by 100+ attendees who are community leaders, business professionals, and advocates. Your name will be in every communication we send leading up to the event. And your team will walk away with a story they'll actually tell. For companies focused on ESG reporting, we can also provide a written impact summary after the event.
"The timing doesn't work — September is too close."
Your Response
Totally fair. That said, commitments can be finalized quickly, and we can accommodate check payments after the event if needed. If September genuinely doesn't work, I'd love to stay in touch — Putts for Progress 2027 will have even more runway for partnerships. Would it be okay if I reached back out early next year?
Timing Reference

When to Send.
When to Wait. When to Move On.

Stage 1 — Day 1
Tuesday through Thursday preferred. 8–10am or 1–3pm local time. Avoid Monday mornings and Friday afternoons.
Wait 5–7 Business Days
If they reply — move to a live conversation. Skip the remaining stages and go straight to a call. If no reply — send Stage 2.
Stage 2 — Day 8–10
Same send-time rules. Keep it short. The goal is a 20-minute call, not a commitment.
Wait 5–7 Business Days
If they reply with interest — schedule the call immediately and send the deck in advance. If no reply — send Stage 3.
Stage 3 — Day 15–18
This is the final touch. Name a specific tier. Include the commitment form link. Close gracefully.
After Stage 3 — Stop
No more follow-up this cycle. Mark the contact inactive for 2026. Set a reminder to re-engage in January 2027 — not a replay of this cadence, a fresh introduction.
Hard Cutoff — August 25
Do not send new Stage 1 emails after August 25. There is not enough runway to complete the full cadence before the September 27 event. Any prospects not yet in the cadence by August 25 move to the 2027 pipeline.
