How to Follow Up
Without Losing the Deal

A structured, multi-touch cadence for corporate sponsor outreach — built around the event timeline and the reality of how corporate giving decisions actually get made.

Why This Exists

Corporate Decisions
Don't Move in a Day

Most sponsorship outreach fails not because the ask was wrong — but because the follow-up stopped too early. Corporate giving coordinators are managing multiple requests at once. A single email rarely closes the deal. What closes the deal is a consistent, well-timed presence that makes the decision easy when the contact is ready.

The average corporate sponsorship decision involves 2–3 internal stakeholders and takes 2–4 weeks from first contact to commitment. This cadence is designed to stay alive through that window without becoming noise.

With the event on September 27, the outreach window opens in late July and closes hard at early September. Every touchpoint below is mapped to that timeline.

The Cadence

Six Touchpoints,
One Clear Goal

Each touchpoint has a specific job. Don't improvise the message — follow the framework. The goal of every touch except the last is to get a reply, not a commitment.

1
Day 1 · Late July Email
The First Impression
Send the sponsor outreach email with the deck attached. This is your only chance to make a cold first impression. Lead with emotion — not features, not tiers. The goal is to earn a reply or a read-through. Do not call the same day.
What to personalize: Reference something specific about their company — a community initiative, a value they've stated publicly, a mutual connection. One sentence of personalization outperforms a perfectly written generic email every time.
Wait 3 days — give them time to open it
2
Day 4 LinkedIn
The Social Signal
Connect with the decision-maker on LinkedIn. Do not pitch in the connection request. Simply connect. If already connected, like or comment on something they've posted recently. This plants your name in their peripheral vision before the follow-up email lands.
Why this works: People are more likely to open a follow-up email from someone whose name they recognize. LinkedIn is recognition, not outreach.
Wait 4 more days
3
Day 8 Email
The Soft Follow-Up
A short, 3–4 sentence email. Do not resend the original. Do not apologize for following up. Reference the first email briefly, add one new piece of value — a stat from the deck, a detail about a specific tier — and end with a single, easy question.
Sample close: "Would a Hole Sponsorship at $500 be a better starting point for this year? Happy to answer any questions before you bring it to the team."
Wait 5 days
4
Day 13 · Mid-August Phone Call
The Human Touch
Call the contact directly. Keep it under 2 minutes. Introduce yourself, reference the emails, and ask one question: "Is this something that might be a fit for your team this year?" If voicemail, leave a message and follow up with a brief email the same day. Do not leave more than one voicemail.
If they answer and say not this year: Thank them, ask if you can follow up in January for 2027. That conversation converts more often than you'd expect.
Wait 7 days
5
Day 20 · Late August Email
The Scarcity Nudge
Club and Fairway spots are limited to 2 each. If either is filling up, say so — truthfully. This email should be the shortest of the sequence: 2–3 sentences, a specific tier, a real availability update, and a direct link to the commitment form at putts4progress.com. No fluff.
Only use scarcity if it's real. If all spots are still available, lead with the event date instead: "The event is four weeks out — here's how to get on the list before it closes."
Wait 10–12 days
6
Day 32 · ~Sep 1–5 Email · Final
The Graceful Close
This is the last outreach before the event. Keep it short and human. Don't pitch — acknowledge the timeline, leave the door open for next year, and make it easy to respond with a single word. Something like: "September 27 is three weeks out — if there's still interest, now is the time. If not, we'd love to have you at the table for 2027."
Why this matters: A graceful close preserves the relationship. You're not chasing — you're wrapping up a conversation professionally. That's what gets a "yes" next year.
When They Respond

How to Handle
Every Reply

A reply at any point in the cadence resets the clock. Stop the sequence and respond within 24 hours. Here's how to read the signal:

✅
They're interested
Send the commitment form link immediately. Direct them to julia@thearcofdfw.org or putts4progress.com/sponsorship-commitment-form. Do not let this stall — move them to action within 24 hours of their reply.
🤔
They have questions
Answer directly and concisely. If they ask about something not in the deck, loop in Julia Chalker. Do not overpromise benefits beyond what's listed on putts4progress.com/sponsorships.
📅
Not this year
Thank them, add them to a 2027 warm list, and set a calendar reminder to reach out in January. A warm "not yet" is worth more than a cold contact next year.
🚫
No reply after Touch 6
Stop outreach. Move them to the 2027 warm list. Do not send a seventh email. Respecting the boundary is what keeps the relationship intact for next year.
Rules of the Road

What the Team
Must Not Do

  • Never promise benefits beyond what is listed at putts4progress.com/sponsorships. If a prospect asks for something not offered, escalate — don't improvise.
  • Never leave more than one voicemail per prospect. A second voicemail signals desperation and will get you ignored.
  • Never use scarcity language unless it's accurate. If spots are still open, say so honestly. False urgency kills credibility.
  • Never follow up more than once after a clear "no." A graceful exit is a future opportunity.
  • Never let an interested reply sit for more than 24 hours. Warm leads go cold fast.
  • Always reference the commitment form at putts4progress.com — don't create friction by asking them to reply via email when a direct link exists.
  • Always log each touchpoint — date, channel, outcome — so the full team knows where each prospect stands.
