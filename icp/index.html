ICP — Audience 01

The Corporate Champion

"Our brand stands for something. This is how we prove it."

Title
Director of Community Affairs, VP of Marketing, HR Director, or Owner
Company Size
Mid-to-large DFW businesses, law firms, financial institutions, healthcare groups
Decision Authority
Controls or heavily influences charitable giving / CSR budget
Geography
DFW Metroplex — Irving, Dallas, Fort Worth, Addison, Plano, Arlington
Give Range
$2,500 – $25,000+ per engagement (event sponsorship, annual partnership)
Trigger
ESG reporting cycles, employee giving season, Q4 budget flush, community visibility goals

Who They Are

The Corporate Champion is a business leader who genuinely believes in giving back — and also needs to be able to justify that giving to a board, a CEO, or a marketing team. They are not cynical; they are accountable. They want to sponsor something that makes their employees proud to show up on Monday, that earns a press mention or social post, and that reflects their company's stated values without requiring them to become an expert in disability advocacy.

They are attracted to organizations that look credible, run tight events, and have a clear story about impact. The Arc's 55+ year history and all-volunteer model signals fiscal responsibility — that message must be surfaced explicitly.

What They Fear

  • Association with an organization that looks disorganized or financially mismanaged
  • Spending CSR budget on something their employees don't connect with or care about
  • Getting "lost in the crowd" — logo on a banner, no real partnership value
  • Difficulty measuring or communicating the ROI of their giving
  • Being asked to commit before they understand what they're buying

What They Need to Hear

  • "Your team will be in the room. Your logo will be seen. Your story will be told."
  • "We've been doing this for 55 years. 100% volunteer-run. Every dollar goes to the work."
  • "Last year's event raised $X. Here's what it funded."
  • "This is what sponsoring at the [Tier] level looks like — in print, online, and on-site."
  • "Your team members can participate directly — not just write a check."

How to Reach Them

  • LinkedIn outreach and LinkedIn event promotion — they live here professionally
  • Personalized sponsorship proposals (not mass email) with tiered packages
  • Warm introductions through existing board members or community connections
  • Dallas Morning News, Dallas Innovates, local chamber of commerce circles
  • Attendance at Chamber of Commerce and DFW nonprofit networking events

ICP — Audience 02

The Heart-Led Giver

"I want to give to something real. Show me the face behind the mission."

Who They Are
DFW residents, 30–65, community-minded professionals or retirees
Connection to IDD
Family member, neighbor, educator, healthcare worker, or personal value alignment
Give Range
$25 – $500 per transaction; recurring donors at $15–$50/month
Event Ticket
$50 General / $75 Tournament — within comfortable discretionary spend
Primary Channel
Facebook, email, word of mouth, community groups, North Texas Giving Day
Conversion Trigger
A story. A face. A specific, tangible impact statement.

Who They Are

The Heart-Led Giver is motivated by meaning, not metrics. They give when they feel something — when a story moves them, when they can imagine the specific person their donation helps. They are often connected to the IDD community through a family member, a student, a neighbor, or simply a lifetime of caring about the vulnerable in their city.

They are loyal when cultivated. A donor who gives $50 to Putts for Progress and receives a genuine follow-up story about impact will give again next year — and bring friends. Their network is their greatest asset for The Arc.

What They Fear

  • Their donation disappearing into overhead with no visible impact
  • Feeling like a number — receiving generic thank-you emails and nothing more
  • Not knowing what The Arc actually does day-to-day
  • Being asked for more money before they feel connected to the mission

What They Need to Hear

  • A specific story about a real person whose life was changed
  • "Your $50 does X." — concrete, visible, human
  • That The Arc is 100% volunteer-run — not a bloated institution
  • An easy, low-pressure way to get involved before being asked to give
  • Recognition that feels personal, not automated

How to Reach Them

  • Facebook — still the dominant platform for community-minded 40–65 demographic in DFW
  • Email newsletter with real stories, not just event announcements
  • North Texas Giving Day (September) — their highest-intent giving moment of the year
  • Word of mouth through existing members and event attendees
  • Nextdoor and local community groups for hyper-local awareness

ICP — Audience 03

The Navigating Family

"We're doing our best with a system that wasn't built for us."

Who They Are
Parents, grandparents, siblings, guardians — or the individuals with IDD themselves
Stage
Early diagnosis to adult transition — navigating IEPs, Medicaid, housing, employment
Primary Need
Information, community, and a trustworthy guide through complex systems
Income Range
Broad — disability does not discriminate by income, though resource access does
Primary Channel
Facebook groups, Navigate Life Texas, word of mouth from schools/therapists/hospitals
Relationship to Arc
Client of advocacy — not a donor, but the reason every donor gives

Who They Are

The Navigating Family is often exhausted, often resilient, and almost always under-informed — not because they aren't trying, but because the systems surrounding disability in Texas are labyrinthine and constantly changing. A mother may discover too late that her son needed to be on the Medicaid waitlist years ago. A grandmother may drive two hours each way for medical care. A caregiver may be burning out because their direct support worker left for a higher-paying job.

The Arc does not provide direct services — but what they provide may be more valuable: knowledge, community, advocacy, and the assurance that someone is fighting at the policy level for their family's future.

What They Fear

  • Not knowing what they don't know — missing deadlines, waitlists, legal windows
  • Being talked down to by organizations that don't actually understand their day-to-day
  • What happens to their family member when they are no longer able to care for them
  • Navigating the transition to adulthood — school ending, services dropping off
  • Isolation — carrying the weight of advocacy alone

What They Need to Hear

  • "You are not alone in this. There is a community here."
  • "Here is information you can actually use — right now, for your situation."
  • "We advocate for your family at every level — local, state, and national."
  • "Your loved one has the right to a self-determined life. We believe that without condition."
  • "You don't have to understand the entire system. You just have to find us first."

Arc Programs Serving This Audience

  • The Self-Determination Group — Run by and for individuals with disabilities (ages 14+). Peer-led, community-driven empowerment.
  • A Look Ahead Conference Series — Expert-led training on special education, transition to adulthood, legal rights, Medicaid, and more.
  • Advocacy Priorities — Fighting for housing, transportation, HCBS funding, civil rights, and health access at the policy level.
  • Story Bank — Amplifying community voices and lived experiences to drive systemic change.
  • Member Network — A community of families, advocates, professionals, and individuals who share resources and support.
