Know Your Prospect

The Corporate Champion
Who They Are & What They Need

Before you send a single email, understand the person on the other end. The Corporate Champion is not cynical — they are accountable. They want to give. They need a reason to justify it, a story to tell their team, and a partner who looks credible enough to stand behind.

Who They Are
Director of Community Affairs, VP of Marketing, HR Director, or Business Owner
Company Size
Mid-to-large DFW businesses — law firms, financial institutions, healthcare groups
Decision Authority
Controls or heavily influences charitable giving / CSR budget
Trigger Moments
ESG reporting cycles, employee giving season, Q4 budget flush, community visibility goals
Geography
DFW Metroplex — Irving, Dallas, Fort Worth, Addison, Plano, Arlington
Give Range
$500 – $2,000 per event sponsorship · Open to annual partnerships
What They Fear
Association with an organization that looks disorganized or financially mismanaged
Spending CSR budget on something their employees won't connect with or care about
Getting "lost in the crowd" — logo on a banner with no real partnership value
Being asked to commit before they understand what they're buying
Difficulty measuring or communicating the ROI of their giving internally
What They Need to Hear
"Your team will be in the room. Your logo will be seen. Your story will be told."
"We've been doing this for 55 years. 100% volunteer-run. Every dollar goes to the work."
"Last year's event raised $51,500 over two years. Here's exactly what it funded."
"This is what sponsoring at your tier looks like — in print, online, and on-site."
"Your team members can participate directly — not just write a check."
Before You Reach Out

Find the Right Person
Not the Right Inbox

Generic info@ and sponsorship@ emails go nowhere. The goal is to find a named stakeholder at each target company — someone with budget authority or community influence — and reach them directly. Use LinkedIn to identify the right contact before sending anything.

Primary Contact — The Decision Maker
Who to find first
The person who controls or influences the CSR / charitable giving budget. They need to feel like the partnership is personally meaningful, not just a line item. Finding their name makes every email land differently.
LinkedIn Search Terms
"Director of Community Affairs" [Company]
"VP Marketing" [Company] DFW
"HR Director" [Company] Irving OR Dallas
"Community Relations" [Company]
"Corporate Social Responsibility" [Company]
Secondary Contact — The Internal Champion
Who to CC or loop in
Someone inside the company who is personally connected to disability, advocacy, or community causes — a parent, a caregiver, a long-term volunteer. When this person receives your email, they become an advocate inside the organization before you ever get a reply.
LinkedIn Search Terms
"Disability" OR "Inclusion" [Company]
"Employee Resource Group" [Company]
"DEI" OR "Diversity" [Company] DFW
"Volunteer" [Company] nonprofit
"Special Needs" OR "IDD" [Company]
Tertiary Contact — The Owner / Principal
For small-to-mid businesses only
At smaller DFW businesses — law firms, medical practices, local financial advisors — the owner is often the decision maker and the community face simultaneously. A personal story lands harder here than any formal sponsorship deck.
LinkedIn Search Terms
"Owner" OR "Principal" [Company] DFW
"Founder" [Company] Irving OR Addison
"Managing Partner" [Company] Dallas
"President" [small company name]
The Cadence

Three Touchpoints.
One Curated Journey.

This is not a blast campaign. Each email reveals a little more of the story, builds a little more context, and earns a little more trust. By the time you make the ask, they already know who The Arc is — and why it matters to their community.

1
Day 1 · First Contact
The Warm Story
Introduce the mission. Make them feel something. Ask for nothing.
Goal: Open the door. This email is not about sponsorship — it's about the people The Arc serves. You are not selling a package. You are inviting them into a story.
To
[First Name] [Last Name] — named stakeholder, not a shared inbox
CC
[Internal Champion] if identified via LinkedIn
Subject
Something is happening in North Texas this September

Dear [First Name],

Somewhere in North Texas right now, there is a young man learning to speak up for himself for the first time. A woman navigating a system that has never been designed with her in mind. A family wondering if the life they want for their child is actually possible.

The Arc of DFW Area exists for them.

For over 55 years — entirely volunteer-run — The Arc has been the organization that shows up when the system doesn't. Fighting for policy change, connecting families to resources they didn't know existed, and building a community where individuals with intellectual and developmental disabilities can live life as they define it.

On September 27th, we're bringing together community leaders, advocates, and businesses at Puttshack in Addison for Putts for Progress — our third annual fundraiser. Every dollar raised goes directly to expanding education, self-advocacy programs, and access to resources for individuals with IDD across 9 DFW counties.

"Putts for Progress isn't just a fundraiser. It's a signal — that businesses in this community believe every person deserves a voice in their own life."

I'd love to share more about what this event means to the families we serve — and what it could mean for [Company Name].

James Lunday
President · The Arc of DFW Area
817.372.3914 · thearcofdfw.org
Guidance Notes for This Email
Personalize the opening with the contact's first name — never "To Whom It May Concern"
If you found an internal champion on LinkedIn, CC them — it signals social proof before the first reply
Do not attach the sponsorship deck in Stage 1 — that comes in Stage 3
Do not include pricing or tier information — this is a story, not a proposal
Send Tuesday through Thursday, 8–10am or 1–3pm — avoid Monday mornings and Friday afternoons
⏳
Wait 5–7 business days. If they reply — move the conversation forward naturally, skip Stage 2 as a formal send, and go straight to the sponsorship conversation. If no reply — send Stage 2.
2
Day 8–10 · Follow-Up
The Relationship Ask
Low pressure. Give them more context. Invite a conversation.
Goal: Surface the opportunity without pressure. You're not asking for a commitment — you're asking for 20 minutes. Make it easy to say yes to a conversation.
To
[First Name] — same thread or new email
Subject
Following up — a partnership worth 20 minutes

[First Name],

I wanted to follow up on my note from last week about Putts for Progress and The Arc of DFW Area.

I know your inbox is full — so I'll be brief. We have a small number of sponsorship opportunities available for our September 27th event at Puttshack in Addison. Based on what [Company Name] stands for in this community, I think there's a meaningful fit here.

Our sponsors don't just write a check. They show up. Their logo is in the room, their name is in every communication we send, and their team gets to be part of an afternoon that matters to hundreds of families across North Texas.

Last year's event raised over $51,500 across our first two years. This year, we're aiming higher — and we want the right partners in the room.

Would any time this week or next work for a 20-minute call? I'd love to walk you through what a partnership could look like for [Company Name] — no pressure, just a conversation.

James Lunday
President · The Arc of DFW Area
817.372.3914 · thearcofdfw.org
Guidance Notes for This Email
Keep it short — they didn't reply to Stage 1, so respect their time; get in and get out
Reference [Company Name] specifically — this is not a template blast, it's a personal follow-up
The ask is a conversation, not a commitment — "20 minutes" is the lowest-friction yes possible
Still no pricing or deck — that comes only after they express interest
If you have a mutual connection, mention it here: "I believe you know [Name] from [Org] — they're a longtime supporter of The Arc."
⏳
Wait 5–7 business days. If they reply and want to talk — schedule the call, send the sponsorship deck ahead of it. If no reply after Stage 2 — send Stage 3, the final touch. If no reply after Stage 3 — mark as inactive and revisit next cycle.
3
Day 15–18 · Final Touch
The Personalized Close
Make the package feel like it was built for them. Give them a clear next step.
Goal: Close the conversation or get a clear no. Present a specific sponsorship tier that aligns with the impact they'd most care about. Make it easy to say yes — and graceful to say not yet.
To
[First Name]
Subject
One last note — and a specific idea for [Company Name]

[First Name],

I don't want to crowd your inbox — so this will be my last note before the event.

I've been thinking about what a partnership between [Company Name] and The Arc of DFW Area could actually look like. Based on [what I know about your company / your focus on community / your team's values], I think the [Recommended Tier] sponsorship is the right fit.

Here's what that means for [Company Name]:

[Insert tier-specific benefits from the close angle below — 3–4 bullet points max, written conversationally, not copy-pasted from the deck]

The full details and commitment form are available at putts4progress.com/sponsorships — or I'm happy to walk you through it directly.

If the timing isn't right this year, I completely understand. I'd still love to stay in touch — the work The Arc does is ongoing, and there will be other opportunities to get involved.

Either way, thank you for your time. The families we serve don't take it for granted — and neither do I.

James Lunday
President · The Arc of DFW Area
817.372.3914 · thearcofdfw.org
Guidance Notes for This Email
Recommend one specific tier — don't present all three options; decisiveness signals confidence
Write the benefits conversationally — pull from the tier close angles below, not the sponsorship deck verbatim
The "not right this year" paragraph is intentional — it keeps the door open and builds goodwill for 2027
Include the commitment form link: putts4progress.com/sponsorships — make it one click to yes
After this email, no more follow-up in this cycle — mark the contact for re-engagement in January 2027

When They Push Back

Objection Handling
What to Say When They Say No

A "no" is rarely final. Most objections are really requests for more information, more time, or a different frame. Use these responses to keep the conversation alive without being pushy.

Objection 01
"Our charitable giving budget is already committed for this year."
Your Response
Completely understood — and I appreciate you being straightforward. If it helps, our commitment form does allow for payment by check, which some partners find easier to route through end-of-year or Q1 budget cycles. Either way, I'd love to keep The Arc of DFW on your radar for 2027 — our event grows every year, and early partnerships tend to get the best visibility. Would it be okay if I reached back out in January?
Objection 02
"We get a lot of sponsorship requests — how is this different?"
Your Response
That's a fair question — and I'll give you a direct answer. Most sponsorships put your logo somewhere and call it done. This one puts your team in a room with families who are navigating one of the hardest things a family can face — and watching your support make that easier. The Arc has been doing this for 55 years, entirely volunteer-run. We don't have overhead eating your donation. And at Putts for Progress, your company isn't a footnote — you're a named partner in a community event that people actually talk about.
Objection 03
"I'm not sure our team has a personal connection to this cause."
Your Response
That's more common than you'd think — and it changes the moment someone meets the community we serve. One in six people in the U.S. has an intellectual or developmental disability. Statistically, someone on your team, or someone they love, is navigating this. What we've found is that when a company shows up for this cause, employees who never mentioned a personal connection come forward. The event tends to mean more than the company expected — and that's usually the start of a longer relationship.
Objection 04
"Can you send us more information first?"
Your Response
Absolutely — I'll send over the sponsorship details now. The full breakdown of packages and benefits is also available at putts4progress.com/sponsorships. Take a look and let me know if a quick call would be helpful to walk through it together — most partners find that 20 minutes answers everything they need to make a decision. I'm happy to work around your schedule.
Objection 05
"What's the ROI on something like this?"
Your Response
Honest answer: the measurable ROI is brand visibility, community association, and employee engagement — which is harder to put a number on than an ad buy, but more durable. What we can tell you: your logo will be seen by 100+ attendees who are community leaders, business professionals, and advocates. Your name will be on every communication we send leading up to the event. And your team will walk away with a story they'll actually tell — which is worth more than most marketing spend. For companies focused on ESG reporting, we're also happy to provide a written impact summary after the event.
Objection 06
"The timing doesn't work — September is too close."
Your Response
Totally fair — the event is September 27th, so the window is short. That said, commitments can be finalized quickly and we can accommodate check payments after the event date if needed. If September genuinely doesn't work, I'd love to stay in touch — The Arc's work continues year-round, and Putts for Progress 2027 will have even more runway for partnerships. Would it be okay if I reached back out early next year?
Timing Reference

When to Send.
When to Wait. When to Move On.

Stage 1 — Send Day 1Tuesday through Thursday preferred. 8–10am or 1–3pm local time. Avoid Monday mornings (full inbox) and Friday afternoons (checked out).
Wait 5–7 business days after Stage 1If they reply at any point — move to a live conversation. Skip the remaining stage emails and go straight to the sponsorship deck and a scheduled call.
Stage 2 — Send Day 8–10Same send-time rules. Keep it short. The goal is a 20-minute call, not a commitment.
Wait 5–7 business days after Stage 2If they reply with interest — schedule the call immediately and send the deck in advance. If no reply — proceed to Stage 3.
Stage 3 — Send Day 15–18This is the final touch. Personalize the tier recommendation. Include the commitment form link. Close gracefully.
After Stage 3 — No More Follow-Up This CycleIf no response after all three stages, mark the contact as inactive for 2026. Add a reminder to re-engage in January 2027 with a fresh relationship-building email — not a replay of this cadence.
Hard Cutoff — August 25Do not send new Stage 1 emails after August 25. There is not enough runway to complete the full cadence before the September 27 event. Any prospects not yet in the cadence by August 25 move to the 2027 pipeline.
